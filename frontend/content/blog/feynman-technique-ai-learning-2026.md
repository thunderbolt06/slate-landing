## What Is the Feynman Technique?

Richard Feynman was a Nobel Prize-winning physicist who believed that if you couldn't explain something simply, you didn't really understand it. His learning method has four steps:

1. Pick a concept you want to learn
2. Explain it as if you're teaching a 12-year-old
3. Identify the gaps where your explanation breaks down
4. Go back to the source material and fill those gaps

It sounds straightforward. But the hardest part has always been step 3 — finding your own blind spots. When you're studying alone, it's easy to convince yourself you understand something you don't.

That's where AI changes everything.

## Step-by-Step: The AI-Assisted Feynman Method

### Step 1: Pick your concept and upload your material

Start with one specific concept from your notes, textbook, or syllabus — not a whole chapter. Upload that material to [Slate](https://slateup.ai) so the AI has the source to work from.

Good candidates:
- A single theorem or proof
- One historical event and its causes
- A biological process (photosynthesis, meiosis)
- A programming concept (recursion, Big O)

### Step 2: Explain it to the AI in plain language

Open a conversation with your AI classmate and say: "I'm going to explain [concept] to you as if you're new to this topic. Tell me where my explanation is wrong or unclear."

Then explain it in the simplest language you can. Don't look at your notes. If you get stuck, that's the point — note where you got stuck.

### Step 3: Let the AI probe your understanding

A good AI won't just say "great job." It will ask:

- "You said X causes Y — can you explain the mechanism?"
- "What would happen if [variable] changed?"
- "How does this connect to [related concept]?"

These questions are the core of the Feynman Technique. They force you to confront the edges of your understanding.

### Step 4: Return to the source and go deeper

When you find a gap, go back to your original material and study that specific point. Then explain it again to the AI. Repeat until your explanation holds up under scrutiny.

This iterative cycle — explain, get questioned, go deeper, explain again — is what makes long-term retention happen.

## Why This Works: The Science

Active recall (retrieving information from memory) produces stronger learning than re-reading or highlighting. The Feynman Technique forces active recall. Studies from cognitive science journals consistently show that students who explain concepts to others outperform those who study passively by a significant margin.

The mechanism is called the "protege effect" — teaching something forces you to organize and encode it more deeply. AI lets you trigger this effect without needing another person.

Combined with spaced repetition (coming back to the same concepts over days and weeks), this approach can dramatically reduce the time needed to master complex material.

The technique works across almost every discipline, but it's particularly powerful for:

**Science and math** — where causal reasoning matters ("why does this formula work, not just what it is")

**History and social sciences** — where understanding context and causation is more important than memorizing dates

**Programming and CS** — where you need to understand not just syntax but the logic underneath

**Medicine and biology** — where systems thinking and interdependencies make rote memorization insufficient

**Economics and business** — where applying frameworks to new scenarios is the real skill

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Using the AI as a search engine** — Don't ask "what is photosynthesis?" Ask the AI to probe your explanation of it. The direction of the teaching matters.

**Accepting vague understanding** — If you can say "I kind of get it" but can't explain it clearly, you don't get it yet. Keep going.

**Skipping hard concepts** — The Feynman method is most valuable on the things you're tempted to skip because they're confusing. Those are exactly the concepts to work through.

**Moving on too fast** — Spend more time per concept than feels comfortable. Depth beats breadth for retention.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is the Feynman Technique better than flashcards?**

They're complementary. Flashcards (especially with spaced repetition tools like Anki) are great for isolated facts. The Feynman Technique is better for understanding concepts and relationships between ideas. Using both together gives you breadth and depth.

**How long should each Feynman session take?**

For one concept, plan 20-40 minutes. That includes your initial explanation, the AI's questioning, going back to source material, and a second explanation. Don't rush it — the discomfort of not knowing is the signal that learning is happening.

**Can I use this for exam prep?**

Yes, and it's particularly effective. Instead of re-reading your notes the night before an exam, spend that time explaining key concepts to an AI. You'll surface gaps that passive review misses, and you'll encode the material more durably.

**What if I can't explain something at all?**

That's fine — and honest. Tell the AI you're starting from zero on a concept and ask it to give you a simple starting point. Then work up from there. The goal is to move from "I can't explain this" to "I can explain this simply" over the course of a session.
